Emphatic Structures in English

This page explains emphatic structures in English, a key grammar area in your C1 English course. Understanding emphatic structures will help you add emphasis and express strong feelings in your speaking and writing, making your language more expressive and nuanced.

Back to Grammar Topics

What Are Emphatic Structures?

Emphatic structures are grammatical patterns used to give special importance or emphasis to particular words, phrases, or ideas in English. They help speakers and writers express strong feelings, contrast ideas, correct misunderstandings, or highlight important information.

Why Use Emphatic Structures?

  • To express strong emotions or feelings
  • To contradict what someone has said
  • To correct a misunderstanding
  • To highlight important information
  • To show contrast between ideas
  • To make your language more expressive and engaging

Comparison

Standard: "I like your presentation."

Emphatic: "I do like your presentation."

Standard: "She finished the project on time."

Emphatic: "What she did was finish the project on time."

Key Concept:

At C1 level, using emphatic structures appropriately demonstrates advanced control of English grammar and helps you express yourself with greater precision and impact. These structures are particularly important in academic writing, presentations, debates, and other formal contexts where nuanced expression is valued.

Emphatic 'Do/Does/Did'

One of the most common ways to add emphasis in English is by using the auxiliary verb 'do/does/did' with the base form of the main verb in affirmative sentences.

Structure

Subject + do/does/did + base form of verb

Present: "I do understand your concerns."

Present (3rd person): "She does know the answer."

Past: "They did arrive on time, despite the traffic."

Common Uses

  • To contradict:
    "You don't like jazz." "I do like jazz!"
  • To insist:
    "I do need your help, please listen to me."
  • To reassure:
    "I do appreciate your efforts, even if I don't always show it."
  • To express surprise:
    "She does look different with her new haircut!"
  • In conditionals:
    "If you do decide to come, let me know."

Pronunciation

When using emphatic 'do/does/did', the auxiliary verb is usually stressed in speech:

  • "I DO understand." (stronger stress on "do")
  • "She DOES know the answer." (stronger stress on "does")
  • "They DID arrive on time." (stronger stress on "did")

Note: In writing, emphatic 'do/does/did' is sometimes italicized to show emphasis, but this is not necessary in formal writing.

Cleft Sentences

Cleft sentences are structures that divide a simple sentence into two parts to emphasize a particular element. There are several types of cleft sentences in English.

It-Cleft Sentences

Structure:

It + be + emphasized element + that/who clause

Original sentence:

"John bought a new car yesterday."

It-cleft versions (emphasizing different elements):

  • Emphasizing the subject: "It was John who bought a new car yesterday."
  • Emphasizing the object: "It was a new car that John bought yesterday."
  • Emphasizing the time: "It was yesterday that John bought a new car."

Wh-Cleft Sentences (Pseudo-Cleft)

Structure:

What/Who/Where/Why/How + clause + be + emphasized element

Original sentence:

"She needs more experience."

Wh-cleft version:

"What she needs is more experience."

More examples:

  • "What I want is a clear explanation."
  • "What happened was the system crashed."
  • "Where we went wrong was in our initial planning."
  • "Why I'm calling is to discuss the proposal."

Reversed Wh-Cleft Sentences

Structure:

Emphasized element + be + what/who/where/why/how + clause

Original sentence:

"She needs more experience."

Reversed wh-cleft version:

More experience is what she needs."

More examples:

  • "A clear explanation is what I want."
  • "The system crashing is what happened."
  • "In our initial planning is where we went wrong."

When to Use Different Cleft Types:

  • It-cleft: Best for emphasizing specific elements (subject, object, time, place)
  • Wh-cleft: Useful for emphasizing actions, reasons, or results
  • Reversed wh-cleft: Effective for creating a dramatic effect or strong conclusion

Fronting and Inversion

Fronting

Fronting involves moving a part of a sentence (usually an object, complement, or adverbial) to the beginning of the sentence for emphasis.

Standard order:

  • "I will never forget that day."
  • "She has read many books on the subject."
  • "They worked extremely hard."

With fronting:

  • "That day I will never forget."
  • "Many books on the subject she has read."
  • "Extremely hard they worked."

Inversion

Inversion involves changing the normal word order of subject and verb (or auxiliary verb) to create emphasis or express a particular meaning.

Types of Inversion:

1. After negative or limiting adverbials:

Standard order:

  • "I have never seen such a beautiful sunset."
  • "She had hardly finished when the phone rang."

With inversion:

  • "Never have I seen such a beautiful sunset."
  • "Hardly had she finished when the phone rang."

Common adverbials that trigger inversion: never, rarely, seldom, hardly, scarcely, no sooner, nowhere, not only, little, only then, not until

2. After certain expressions with "so" and "such":

Standard order:

  • "The weather was so bad that we stayed home."

With inversion:

  • "So bad was the weather that we stayed home."

3. In conditional sentences without "if":

Standard order:

  • "If I had known earlier, I would have helped."

With inversion:

  • "Had I known earlier, I would have helped."

Important Note:

Fronting and inversion are advanced grammatical techniques that add sophistication to your English. However, they should be used sparingly and in appropriate contexts. Overuse can make your language sound unnatural or overly dramatic.

Other Emphatic Structures

Emphatic Reflexive Pronouns

Reflexive pronouns (myself, yourself, himself, etc.) can be used for emphasis.

Examples:

  • "I myself saw the accident happen."
  • "The president himself attended the ceremony."
  • "They built the house themselves."

The "All" Structure

Using "all" at the beginning of a sentence for emphasis.

Examples:

  • "All I want is a cup of tea."
  • "All you need to do is sign here."
  • "All she did was ask a simple question."

Repetition

Repeating words or phrases for emphasis.

Examples:

  • "It was a long, long time ago."
  • "We need to work faster and faster."
  • "The situation is getting worse and worse."

Emphatic Tags

Adding tags at the end of sentences for emphasis.

Examples:

  • "It's a great film, that one."
  • "She's very talented, your sister."
  • "It's difficult to understand, this concept."

Intensifiers and Emphasizers

Using certain adverbs to add emphasis.

Common intensifiers and emphasizers:

  • Really, very, extremely, incredibly: "That was really helpful."
  • Absolutely, completely, totally: "I'm absolutely certain."
  • Indeed, certainly, definitely: "She certainly deserved the award."
  • Actually, in fact, as a matter of fact: "I actually finished it early."
  • Particularly, especially, specifically: "I'm particularly interested in this topic."

Common Mistakes with Emphatic Structures

Incorrect: "It was in Paris where we met."

Correct: "It was in Paris that we met." OR "Paris is where we met."

Remember: In it-cleft sentences, use "that" for places, things, and times (not "where," "which," or "when").

Incorrect: "Never I have seen such a beautiful sunset."

Correct: "Never have I seen such a beautiful sunset."

Remember: With negative adverbials at the beginning, invert the subject and auxiliary verb.

Incorrect: "What I want is that you help me."

Correct: "What I want is for you to help me." OR "What I want is your help."

Remember: After "What I want is..." use a noun phrase or "for + object + to-infinitive" (not a that-clause).

Incorrect: "I do can help you."

Correct: "I can help you." OR "I do want to help you."

Remember: Don't use emphatic "do" with modal verbs or auxiliary verbs.

Incorrect: "The president himself's speech was inspiring."

Correct: "The president's speech itself was inspiring." OR "The president himself gave an inspiring speech."

Remember: Place emphatic reflexive pronouns after the noun they refer to or after the verb.
