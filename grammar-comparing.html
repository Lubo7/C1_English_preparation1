Grammar Topic 8: Comparing and Contrasting

This page provides a comprehensive overview of comparing and contrasting structures in English, including comparative and superlative forms, advanced comparative structures, and parallel structures.

Comparative and Superlative Forms

Regular Adjectives

Adjective Comparative Superlative
Short adjectives (1 syllable)
e.g., cheap, tall, fast		 cheaper, taller, faster
(add -er)		 the cheapest, the tallest, the fastest
(add -est)
Adjectives ending in -e
e.g., nice, large		 nicer, larger
(add -r)		 the nicest, the largest
(add -st)
Adjectives ending in consonant + y
e.g., happy, easy		 happier, easier
(change y to i, add -er)		 the happiest, the easiest
(change y to i, add -est)
Short adjectives ending in consonant-vowel-consonant
e.g., hot, big		 hotter, bigger
(double final consonant, add -er)		 the hottest, the biggest
(double final consonant, add -est)
Longer adjectives (2+ syllables)
e.g., expensive, difficult		 more expensive, more difficult the most expensive, the most difficult

Irregular Adjectives

Adjective Comparative Superlative
good better the best
bad worse the worst
far farther/further the farthest/furthest
little less the least
many/much more the most
old older/elder the oldest/eldest

Examples

  • This exercise is easier than I expected.
  • She is the best student in the class.
  • The weather today is worse than yesterday.
  • This is the most interesting book I have ever read.
  • The exam was more challenging than we thought it would be.

Advanced Comparative Structures

The more... the more...

This structure is used to show how one change leads to another change.

  • The more you practice, the more you improve.
  • The harder you work, the better results you'll get.
  • The less time you spend studying, the worse your grades will be.
  • The more I learn about this subject, the more fascinating I find it.

Twice/Three times/Half as... as...

These structures are used for precise comparisons of quantity or degree.

  • This book is twice as expensive as that one.
  • The new office is three times as large as the old one.
  • The journey took half as long as we expected.
  • This course is four times as comprehensive as the previous one.

Not as/so... as...

This structure is used for negative comparisons.

  • This exercise is not as difficult as the previous one.
  • The film was not so good as I had hoped.
  • The hotel was not as expensive as we had feared.
  • The results were not as impressive as last year's.

Modifiers with Comparatives

These words and phrases are used to modify the degree of comparison.

  • Far/much/a lot/considerably + comparative: This course is far more comprehensive than the other one.
  • Slightly/a bit/a little + comparative: The new version is slightly better than the old one.
  • No + comparative: The second test was no more difficult than the first.
  • Any + comparative: Is the new restaurant any better than the old one?
  • Even + comparative: The traffic was even worse than yesterday.

Parallel Structures

What Are Parallel Structures?

Parallel structure means using the same grammatical form for elements being compared or listed. This creates balance and clarity in your writing and speaking.

Examples of Parallel Structure

Not Parallel Parallel
She enjoys reading books, to watch movies, and playing games. She enjoys reading books, watching movies, and playing games.
The teacher asked us to read the chapter, that we should take notes, and to prepare questions. The teacher asked us to read the chapter, take notes, and prepare questions.
I would rather walk than taking the bus. I would rather walk than take the bus.
The advantages of studying abroad include learning a new language, cultural experiences, and you can make international friends. The advantages of studying abroad include learning a new language, experiencing different cultures, and making international friends.

Parallel Structure in Comparisons

When comparing two things, it's important to maintain parallel structure.

  • Living in the city is more expensive than living in the countryside.
  • I prefer studying in the morning to studying at night.
  • She is better at writing essays than giving presentations.
  • The new system is not only more efficient but also more user-friendly.

Common Mistakes

  • Double comparatives: "more better" (incorrect) → "better" or "more good" (correct)
  • Double superlatives: "most biggest" (incorrect) → "biggest" or "most big" (correct)
  • Incorrect form for long/short adjectives: "expensiver" (incorrect) → "more expensive" (correct)
  • Mixing "as" and "than": "not as good than" (incorrect) → "not as good as" (correct)
  • Lack of parallelism: "She likes swimming more than to run" (incorrect) → "She likes swimming more than running" (correct)
  • Incorrect use of articles with superlatives: "He is best student" (incorrect) → "He is the best student" (correct)
