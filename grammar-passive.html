Passive Structures in English

This page explains passive voice structures in English, a key grammar area in your C1 English course. Understanding passive structures will help you express ideas with different emphasis and in various formal contexts accurately in your exam.

Back to Grammar Topics

Active vs. Passive Voice

In English, there are two "voices" - active and passive. The voice indicates whether the subject of the sentence performs the action (active) or receives the action (passive).

Active Voice

In active voice, the subject performs the action expressed by the verb.

Structure:

Subject + Verb + Object

Examples:

  • The chef prepares the meals.
  • Scientists have discovered a new species.
  • The company will announce the results tomorrow.

Passive Voice

In passive voice, the subject receives the action expressed by the verb.

Structure:

Subject + be + Past Participle (+ by + agent)

Examples:

  • The meals are prepared by the chef.
  • A new species has been discovered by scientists.
  • The results will be announced tomorrow.

Key Differences:

  • In active voice, the focus is on the doer of the action
  • In passive voice, the focus is on the receiver of the action
  • The agent (doer) in passive voice is often omitted if it's unknown or unimportant
  • Passive voice is often used in formal, scientific, or academic writing

Forming the Passive Voice

The passive voice is formed using the appropriate form of the verb "to be" followed by the past participle of the main verb.

Tense Active Voice Passive Voice
Present Simple They build houses. Houses are built (by them).
Present Continuous They are building a house. A house is being built (by them).
Present Perfect They have built a house. A house has been built (by them).
Past Simple They built a house. A house was built (by them).
Past Continuous They were building a house. A house was being built (by them).
Past Perfect They had built a house. A house had been built (by them).
Future Simple They will build a house. A house will be built (by them).
Future Perfect They will have built a house. A house will have been built (by them).
Modal Verbs They should build a house. A house should be built (by them).

Note:

Some tenses are rarely used in the passive voice, particularly the continuous perfect forms (e.g., present perfect continuous, past perfect continuous). For example, "The house has been being built" sounds awkward and is usually avoided.

When to Use the Passive Voice

Common Uses

  • When the doer is unknown:
    "My car was stolen last night." (We don't know who stole it)
  • When the doer is obvious or unimportant:
    "The road is being repaired." (It's obvious that road workers are doing it)
  • When the focus is on the action or recipient:
    "The Mona Lisa was painted by Leonardo da Vinci." (Focus on the painting)
  • In formal or scientific writing:
    "The experiment was conducted under controlled conditions."
  • To avoid mentioning the doer:
    "Mistakes were made." (Avoiding responsibility)
  • For emphasis or dramatic effect:
    "Justice was finally served."

Common Contexts

  • Academic writing:
    "It is generally accepted that climate change is occurring."
  • Scientific reports:
    "The samples were analyzed using spectroscopy."
  • News reporting:
    "The suspect was arrested yesterday."
  • Legal documents:
    "The defendant was found guilty."
  • Business communications:
    "Your application has been received."
  • Instructions and processes:
    "The button should be pressed to start the machine."

Advanced Passive Structures

Passive with Reporting Verbs

Verbs like say, believe, think, report, know, expect, etc. can be used in special passive constructions:

Active:

People believe that he is innocent.

Passive (two forms):

  1. It is believed that he is innocent.
  2. He is believed to be innocent.

More examples:

  • They say that the president will resign. →
    It is said that the president will resign. / The president is said to be resigning.
  • People expect that the prices will rise. →
    It is expected that the prices will rise. / The prices are expected to rise.

Passive with Infinitives and Gerunds

Infinitives and gerunds can also be used in the passive voice:

Active infinitive:

I expect someone to clean the room.

Passive infinitive:

I expect the room to be cleaned.

Active gerund:

I remember someone telling me about it.

Passive gerund:

I remember being told about it.

More examples:

  • The manager wants someone to finish this project. →
    The manager wants this project to be finished.
  • She enjoys people admiring her work. →
    She enjoys her work being admired.

Passive with Modal Verbs

Modal verbs can be combined with the passive voice to express various meanings:

Obligation/Necessity:

  • The form must be completed by tomorrow.
  • This issue should be addressed immediately.
  • The rules have to be followed by all participants.

Possibility/Probability:

  • The package might be delivered today.
  • The problem could be solved with this approach.
  • The document may have been misplaced.

Verbs with Two Objects in Passive Voice

Some verbs can take two objects (direct and indirect). In such cases, either object can become the subject of a passive sentence.

Active:

They gave me a book.

Passive (two possibilities):

  1. I was given a book (by them).
  2. A book was given to me (by them).

Common verbs that follow this pattern include: give, offer, send, show, teach, tell, promise, sell, lend, etc.

More examples:

  • They offered her a job. →
    She was offered a job. / A job was offered to her.
  • Someone sent him an invitation. →
    He was sent an invitation. / An invitation was sent to him.

Note:

In modern English, especially in formal contexts, it's more common to make the indirect object (the person) the subject of the passive sentence.

"I was given a book" is more common than "A book was given to me"

Get Passive

In informal English, "get" is sometimes used instead of "be" to form the passive voice, especially when talking about things that happen to people.

Get Passive Structure

Structure:

Subject + get + past participle

Examples:

  • He got invited to the party.
  • They got married last year.
  • I got fired from my job.
  • She got promoted to manager.

When to Use Get Passive

  • For unexpected events:
    "My phone got stolen yesterday."
  • For negative experiences:
    "He got hurt in the accident."
  • For changes in state or situation:
    "They got engaged last month."
  • In informal speech:
    "I got confused by his explanation."

Note: The "get passive" is less formal than the "be passive" and is more common in spoken English than in formal writing.

Common Mistakes with Passive Voice

Incorrect: The book was written by Shakespeare in 1623.

Correct: The book was written in 1623 by Shakespeare.

Remember: In passive voice, place time expressions before the agent (by + doer).

Incorrect: The window was breaking by the children.

Correct: The window was broken by the children.

Remember: Use the past participle (not the -ing form) in passive voice.

Incorrect: My car has stolen last night.

Correct: My car was stolen last night.

Remember: Use the appropriate form of "be" (not "have") in passive voice.

Incorrect: This problem can't solved easily.

Correct: This problem can't be solved easily.

Remember: Include "be" after modal verbs in passive voice.

Incorrect: I was explaining the rules by the teacher.

Correct: The rules were explained to me by the teacher.

Remember: The object of the active sentence becomes the subject of the passive sentence.
