Causative Structures in English

This page explains causative structures in English, a key grammar area in your C1 English course. Understanding causatives will help you express situations where someone causes something to happen through another person accurately in your exam.

Back to Grammar Topics

What Are Causative Structures?

Causative structures are used to show that a person or thing is causing something to happen. In English, we use these structures when someone arranges for another person to do something for them, rather than doing it themselves.

Key Concept

Causative structures express the idea that "X causes Y to happen" or "X causes Y to do something." The causer doesn't perform the action directly but arranges for it to be done.

Comparison

Direct action: "I cleaned my car."

Causative: "I had my car cleaned." (Someone else did the cleaning)

Why Use Causatives?

  • To show that someone arranged for a service
  • To indicate that someone else performed an action for the subject
  • To express that the subject made something happen without doing it personally
  • To describe situations where the subject lacks the ability or time to do something themselves

Have Something Done

The most common causative structure is "have something done," which is used when we arrange for someone else to do something for us.

Structure

Subject + have + object + past participle (+ by + agent)

Example: "I had my hair cut yesterday."

Meaning: I arranged for someone (a hairdresser) to cut my hair.

Tense Forms

  • Present Simple:
    "I have my car serviced every year."
  • Present Continuous:
    "I'm having my house painted this week."
  • Past Simple:
    "She had her laptop repaired last month."
  • Present Perfect:
    "They have had their roof fixed recently."
  • Future:
    "We will have our garden landscaped next spring."

Common Uses

  • Professional services:
    "I'm having my teeth checked tomorrow."
  • Home improvements:
    "They had their kitchen renovated."
  • Repairs:
    "He's having his watch fixed."
  • Personal services:
    "She has her nails done every two weeks."
  • Administrative tasks:
    "We had our documents translated."

Important Note:

The agent (the person who performs the action) is often omitted because it's either obvious or unimportant. However, you can include it using "by":

"I had my suit tailored by an expert seamstress."

Get Something Done

"Get something done" is an alternative to "have something done" and is more common in informal English.

Structure

Subject + get + object + past participle

Example: "I got my phone screen replaced."

Meaning: I arranged for someone to replace my phone screen.

Examples in Different Tenses

  • Present Simple:
    "I always get my tires checked before a long trip."
  • Present Continuous:
    "She's getting her eyes tested today."
  • Past Simple:
    "We got our visas processed last week."
  • Present Perfect:
    "Have you got your application submitted yet?"
  • Future:
    "I'll get my hair styled before the wedding."

Have vs. Get

  • Formality:
    "Have something done" is more formal than "get something done"
  • Effort:
    "Get something done" sometimes implies more effort or difficulty
  • Usage:
    "Get" is more common in spoken English and informal writing
  • Regional differences:
    "Get" is particularly common in American English

Note: Both forms are correct and widely used. In formal writing or exams, "have something done" may be preferred.

Other Causative Verbs

Besides "have" and "get," several other verbs can be used in causative structures, each with slightly different meanings.

Make

Structure:

Subject + make + object + base form (without "to")

Meaning:

Force or require someone to do something

Examples:

  • The teacher made the students repeat the exercise.
  • Her story made everyone laugh.
  • The rain made us cancel our picnic.

Let

Structure:

Subject + let + object + base form (without "to")

Meaning:

Allow or permit someone to do something

Examples:

  • My parents let me stay out until midnight.
  • The teacher let the students use dictionaries during the test.
  • She won't let anyone touch her collection.

Help

Structure:

Subject + help + object + (to) base form

Meaning:

Assist someone in doing something

Examples:

  • She helped me (to) carry the boxes upstairs.
  • The medication helped him (to) sleep better.
  • Can you help me (to) translate this document?

Note: "To" is optional after "help" in modern English.

Have (someone do something)

Structure:

Subject + have + object + base form (without "to")

Meaning:

Arrange for or instruct someone to do something

Examples:

  • I'll have my assistant call you tomorrow.
  • The manager had everyone work overtime.
  • She had her son clean his room before going out.

Causative Verbs with To-Infinitive

Some causative verbs are followed by the to-infinitive rather than the base form.

Ask, Tell, Advise, Encourage, Persuade, Remind

Structure:

Subject + verb + object + to + base form

Examples:

  • She asked me to help her with the project.
  • The doctor told him to take the medicine twice a day.
  • My friend advised me to apply for the job.
  • Parents should encourage their children to read more.
  • He persuaded her to change her mind.
  • Please remind me to call my mother later.

Want, Need, Would Like, Expect

Structure:

Subject + verb + object + to + base form

Examples:

  • I want you to be honest with me.
  • They need everyone to arrive on time.
  • I would like you to consider my proposal.
  • The teacher expects all students to complete the assignment by Friday.

Note: These verbs express desire or necessity for someone else to do something, which is a form of causation.

Negative and Question Forms

Negative Forms

Have/Get Something Done:

  • I didn't have my hair cut yesterday.
  • She hasn't had her car repaired yet.
  • We don't get our windows cleaned very often.

Make/Let:

  • The teacher didn't make us finish the entire test.
  • My parents don't let me stay out after midnight.

Verbs with To-Infinitive:

  • She didn't ask me to help her.
  • I don't want you to worry about it.

Question Forms

Have/Get Something Done:

  • Did you have your hair cut yesterday?
  • Has she had her car repaired yet?
  • How often do you get your windows cleaned?

Make/Let:

  • Did the teacher make you finish the entire test?
  • Do your parents let you stay out after midnight?

Verbs with To-Infinitive:

  • Did she ask you to help her?
  • Do you want me to pick you up?

Common Mistakes with Causative Structures

Incorrect: I had cut my hair yesterday.

Correct: I had my hair cut yesterday.

Remember: In causative structures with "have," the object comes before the past participle.

Incorrect: She made him to clean the room.

Correct: She made him clean the room.

Remember: After "make" and "let," use the base form without "to."

Incorrect: I will have my assistant to call you.

Correct: I will have my assistant call you.

Remember: When "have" means "arrange for someone to do something," use the base form without "to."

Incorrect: They got repair their car.

Correct: They got their car repaired.

Remember: In causative structures with "get," the object comes before the past participle.

Incorrect: The teacher let the students to use dictionaries.

Correct: The teacher let the students use dictionaries.

Remember: After "let," use the base form without "to."
