Future Forms in English

This page explains the different ways to express future time in English, a key grammar area in your C1 English course. Understanding these forms will help you express future plans, predictions, and arrangements accurately in your exam.

Back to Grammar Topics

Overview of Future Forms

English has several different ways to talk about the future, each with specific uses and connotations. At C1 level, you need to understand the nuances between these forms and use them appropriately in different contexts.

Future Form Structure Main Uses
Will will + base form Predictions, spontaneous decisions, promises, offers
Going to be going to + base form Plans, intentions, predictions based on present evidence
Present Continuous be + verb-ing Fixed arrangements, scheduled events
Present Simple base form (or 3rd person -s) Timetables, schedules, programs
Future Continuous will be + verb-ing Actions in progress at a specific time in the future
Future Perfect will have + past participle Actions completed before a specific time in the future
Future Perfect Continuous will have been + verb-ing Actions continuing up to a specific time in the future

Will and Going To

Will

We use 'will' for:

  • Predictions about the future without present evidence:
    "I think it will rain tomorrow."
  • Spontaneous decisions made at the moment of speaking:
    "I'll help you with that."
  • Promises or personal intentions:
    "I'll call you as soon as I arrive."
  • Offers of help or assistance:
    "I'll carry that bag for you."
  • Requests (often with 'will you'):
    "Will you close the window, please?"
  • Refusals (often with 'won't'):
    "The car won't start this morning."

Going To

We use 'be going to' for:

  • Plans and intentions decided before the moment of speaking:
    "I'm going to study medicine next year."
  • Predictions based on present evidence:
    "Look at those clouds! It's going to rain."
  • Actions that are about to happen:
    "Be careful! The vase is going to fall!"
  • Inevitable events that are certain to happen:
    "The population is going to reach 10 billion by 2050."

Note: 'Will' and 'going to' can sometimes be used interchangeably, but they often convey subtle differences in meaning. 'Going to' typically suggests more certainty or evidence, while 'will' can be more spontaneous or speculative.

Present Tenses for Future

Present Continuous for Future

We use the present continuous for:

  • Fixed arrangements that are planned and organized:
    "I'm meeting Sarah at the airport tomorrow."
  • Personal arrangements with other people:
    "We're having dinner with the Johnsons on Saturday."
  • Future plans that are certain and decided:
    "I'm starting my new job next week."

Note: The present continuous for future always requires a future time expression (tomorrow, next week, on Saturday) or the future meaning must be clear from the context.

Present Simple for Future

We use the present simple for:

  • Timetables and schedules (trains, planes, classes, etc.):
    "The train leaves at 8:30 tomorrow morning."
  • Programs and calendars (events that are part of an official arrangement):
    "The conference begins on Monday."
  • Fixed events that are not controlled by people:
    "The sun rises at 5:42 AM tomorrow."

Note: The present simple is used for future events that are seen as facts, not as plans or intentions. These events are usually part of a fixed schedule that is not easily changed.

Advanced Future Forms

Future Continuous

We use the future continuous (will be + verb-ing) for:

  • Actions in progress at a specific time in the future:
    "This time tomorrow, I'll be sitting on the beach in Spain."
  • Ongoing activities that will happen in the normal course of events:
    "I'll be working late tonight."
  • Polite enquiries about someone's plans:
    "Will you be using the car this evening?"
  • Predicted activities that are expected to happen:
    "I expect they'll be arriving around 8 PM."

Future Perfect

We use the future perfect (will have + past participle) for:

  • Actions completed before a specific time in the future:
    "By this time next year, I will have graduated from university."
  • Predictions about what will have happened by a certain time:
    "Scientists will have found a cure for many types of cancer by 2050."
  • Looking back from a future point in time:
    "By the end of this month, I will have worked here for five years."

Future Perfect Continuous

We use the future perfect continuous (will have been + verb-ing) for:

  • Actions continuing up to a specific time in the future:
    "By next month, I will have been studying English for ten years."
  • Emphasizing the duration of an activity in the future:
    "By the time you arrive, I will have been waiting for three hours."
  • Explaining consequences of a future continuous action:
    "I'll be tired tomorrow because I will have been working all night."

Note: The future perfect continuous emphasizes the duration or ongoing nature of an action up to a future time, while the future perfect emphasizes the completion of an action by a future time.

Other Ways to Express Future

Be About To

We use 'be about to' for:

  • Imminent actions that will happen very soon:
    "The plane is about to take off."
  • Actions on the point of happening:
    "I was about to call you when you texted me."

Be Due To

We use 'be due to' for:

  • Scheduled events expected to happen at a specific time:
    "The train is due to arrive at 3:15 PM."
  • Expected events based on a schedule or plan:
    "The new shopping center is due to open next month."

Be To + Infinitive

We use 'be to + infinitive' for:

  • Formal arrangements, often in official contexts:
    "The President is to visit France next week."
  • Instructions or orders:
    "You are to report to the manager immediately."
  • Formal plans:
    "The new policy is to be implemented next month."

Be Set To / Be Bound To

We use these expressions for:

  • Be set to: Events expected to happen based on current circumstances:
    "The company is set to announce record profits this year."
  • Be bound to: Events that are certain or inevitable:
    "With all this traffic, we're bound to be late."

Common Mistakes with Future Forms

Incorrect: I will meeting you tomorrow.

Correct: I will meet you tomorrow. OR I am meeting you tomorrow.

Remember: After 'will', use the base form of the verb without 'to'.

Incorrect: I am going to to visit my parents.

Correct: I am going to visit my parents.

Remember: After 'going to', use the base form of the verb without 'to'.

Incorrect: I will to call you later.

Correct: I will call you later.

Remember: 'Will' is followed by the base form without 'to'.

Incorrect: By next year, I will worked here for ten years.

Correct: By next year, I will have worked here for ten years.

Remember: For actions completed before a future time, use the future perfect (will have + past participle).

Incorrect: This time tomorrow I will flying to Paris.

Correct: This time tomorrow I will be flying to Paris.

Remember: For actions in progress at a future time, use the future continuous (will be + verb-ing).
