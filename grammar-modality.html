Modality in English

This page explains modal verbs and expressions in English, a key grammar area in your C1 English course. Understanding modality will help you express degrees of certainty, obligation, permission, and ability accurately in your exam.

Back to Grammar Topics

Overview of Modal Verbs

Modal verbs are auxiliary verbs that express modality â€“ that is, they indicate the speaker's attitude toward the action or state described by the main verb. They express concepts such as possibility, probability, necessity, obligation, permission, ability, and advice.

Modal Verb Main Uses Example
Can Ability, possibility, permission I can speak three languages.
Could Past ability, possibility, polite requests Could you help me with this?
May Possibility, permission (formal) It may rain later today.
Might Possibility (less certain than may) She might not come to the party.
Must Obligation, necessity, strong probability You must submit your application by Friday.
Should Advice, recommendation, expectation You should exercise regularly.
Will Future actions, predictions, promises I will call you tomorrow.
Would Hypothetical situations, polite requests Would you mind opening the window?
Shall Suggestions, offers, formal future (British English) Shall we go for a walk?
Ought to Advice, moral obligation (similar to should) You ought to apologize to her.

Key Characteristics of Modal Verbs:

  • They are followed by the base form of the verb without 'to'
  • They do not change form for different subjects (no -s in third person singular)
  • They don't have infinitive or -ing forms
  • They form questions and negatives without 'do'
  • They are used with 'have + past participle' to refer to the past

Expressing Ability

Can and Could

We use 'can' and 'could' to express ability:

  • Present ability:
    "I can speak French fluently."
  • Past ability:
    "When I was younger, I could run much faster."
  • Future ability (with 'be able to'):
    "After the course, I will be able to communicate in Spanish."

Be Able To

We use 'be able to' when we need forms that 'can' doesn't have:

  • Infinitive:
    "I want to be able to speak Japanese by next year."
  • Perfect forms:
    "She has been able to solve complex problems since childhood."
  • Future forms:
    "They will be able to attend the conference next month."
  • Specific achievement in the past:
    "Despite the difficulties, we were able to complete the project on time." (not "could complete")

Expressing Possibility and Probability

May and Might

We use 'may' and 'might' to express possibility:

  • May (50-70% certainty):
    "She may arrive late due to traffic."
  • Might (30-50% certainty):
    "It might rain later, but I'm not sure."
  • Past possibility:
    "They may/might have missed their flight."

Note: In modern English, the difference between 'may' and 'might' is often subtle. 'Might' can suggest a slightly lower degree of possibility than 'may'.

Could and Can

We also use 'could' and 'can' to express possibility:

  • Could (similar to 'might'):
    "This could be the solution to our problem."
  • Past possibility:
    "She could have forgotten about the meeting."
  • Can (general possibility):
    "Accidents can happen to anyone."
  • Cannot/can't (impossibility):
    "That can't be true! He was with me at the time."

Must and Can't for Deduction

We use 'must' and 'can't' to express logical deduction:

  • Must (positive deduction, 90-100% certainty):
    "You must be tired after such a long journey." (I'm almost certain you are tired)
  • Can't/couldn't (negative deduction, 90-100% certainty):
    "She can't be at home. I just saw her at the supermarket." (I'm almost certain she is not at home)
  • Past deduction:
    "He must have left early." / "They can't have known about the change in plans."

Expressing Obligation and Necessity

Must and Have To

We use 'must' and 'have to' to express obligation:

  • Must (personal obligation, speaker's authority):
    "I must finish this report today." (I'm imposing this on myself)
  • Have to (external obligation):
    "I have to wear a uniform at work." (The company requires it)
  • Past obligation:
    "I had to work late yesterday." (not "must")
  • Future obligation:
    "You will have to submit your application by next week."

Note: In negative forms, 'must not' and 'don't have to' have very different meanings:
- "You must not tell anyone" = prohibition (don't do it)
- "You don't have to tell anyone" = lack of obligation (it's your choice)

Should, Ought To, and Need To

We use these modals for less strong obligation or necessity:

  • Should/Ought to (advice, recommendation):
    "You should exercise regularly."
    "You ought to see a doctor about that cough."
  • Need to (necessity):
    "I need to buy some groceries."
  • Past forms:
    "You should have told me earlier." (but you didn't)
    "I needed to finish the project yesterday."

Be Supposed To

We use 'be supposed to' to express expectation or arrangement:

  • Expected behavior:
    "You're supposed to turn off your phone during the performance."
  • Arrangement:
    "The delivery is supposed to arrive before noon."
  • Unfulfilled expectation (past):
    "They were supposed to call, but they didn't."

Expressing Permission and Prohibition

Can, Could, and May

We use these modals to express permission:

  • Can (informal permission):
    "You can use my laptop if you need to."
  • Could (more polite request for permission):
    "Could I leave early today?"
  • May (formal permission):
    "May I come in?"
    "Students may use the library until 10 PM."

Must Not, Can't, and May Not

We use these modals to express prohibition:

  • Must not/Mustn't (strong prohibition):
    "Visitors must not feed the animals."
  • Can't/Cannot (prohibition, often rules or laws):
    "You can't park here between 8 AM and 6 PM."
  • May not (formal prohibition):
    "Candidates may not bring mobile phones into the examination room."

Be Allowed To

We use 'be allowed to' when we need forms that 'can' doesn't have:

  • Past permission:
    "We were allowed to leave early yesterday."
  • Future permission:
    "Visitors will be allowed to take photographs in the museum starting next month."
  • Perfect forms:
    "They haven't been allowed to access that information."

Modal Perfects

Modal perfects (modal verb + have + past participle) are used to talk about the past. They often express speculation about past events or criticize past actions.

Speculation about the Past

  • Must have (certainty):
    "She must have arrived by now." (I'm almost certain she has arrived)
  • May/Might have (possibility):
    "They might have missed their train." (It's possible they missed it)
  • Could have (possibility):
    "He could have forgotten about our meeting." (It's possible he forgot)
  • Can't/Couldn't have (impossibility):
    "She can't have seen me. She was looking the other way." (I'm certain she didn't see me)

Criticism or Regret

  • Should have (advisable action not taken):
    "You should have called me." (but you didn't)
  • Shouldn't have (inadvisable action taken):
    "I shouldn't have eaten so much." (but I did)
  • Could have (missed opportunity):
    "We could have won the match." (but we didn't)
  • Needn't have (unnecessary action taken):
    "You needn't have bought so much food." (but you did)

Important Distinctions:

  • Didn't need to vs. Needn't have:
    - "I didn't need to finish the report yesterday." (and I didn't do it)
    - "I needn't have finished the report yesterday." (but I did do it, unnecessarily)
  • Could have vs. Was/Were able to:
    - "I could have attended the meeting." (but I didn't)
    - "I was able to attend the meeting." (and I did)

Common Mistakes with Modal Verbs

Incorrect: I must to go now.

Correct: I must go now.

Remember: Modal verbs are followed by the base form without 'to'.

Incorrect: She cans speak French.

Correct: She can speak French.

Remember: Modal verbs don't change form for different subjects.

Incorrect: I don't must work tomorrow.

Correct: I don't have to work tomorrow. OR I mustn't work tomorrow.

Remember: 'Must' forms negatives without 'do'. 'Don't have to' means no obligation, while 'mustn't' means prohibition.

Incorrect: I will can drive after I get my license.

Correct: I will be able to drive after I get my license.

Remember: Two modal verbs cannot be used together. Use 'be able to' after 'will'.

Incorrect: You should to visit Paris.

Correct: You should visit Paris.

Remember: Modal verbs are followed by the base form without 'to'.
